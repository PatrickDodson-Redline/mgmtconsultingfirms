Professional services firms grow by winning work and expanding from it, so the cycle is a loop. Redline Growth applies across the whole cycle, with a continuous relationship layer wrapping the active pursuit: present before issues are defined, and through execution and expansion.

Monitor · Before issues are defined
Be present, and get known where it counts
Problem. We're known where we've worked and invisible where we haven't. And the accounts we'd most want are the ones we have no line into. By the time an RFP posts, the firms in the relationship are already on the list and we're not.
Solution.
  • Keep a steady presence with target accounts, in your growth leaders' voices.
  • Most future clients are not buying now: about 5 percent are in-market in a quarter, twenty percent in a year.
  • Concentrate relationship-building on the names your signals put nearest the buying window.
  • Map your growth leaders' first and second-degree networks to find who sits one introduction from each target.
Outcome. Engagement is established proactively, when you have the greatest opportunity to help surface needs and shape the approach. You can shape the RFP, or avoid it altogether by being in front of it.
The buying cycle · the active pursuit
Detect
See which accounts are moving
Problem. By the time we hear a client or a target is in motion, someone is already in the room.
Solution.
  • Watch your client base and target list for events that signal a buying cycle.
  • Triggers: a new mandate, a leadership change, a recapitalization, or a competitive loss.
  • Tune to your own experience and first-party data on what spurs clients to act.
Outcome. You learn an account is moving while there is still time to influence it or gain the inside position on a competitive bid.
Translate
Spend growth leader time where it converts
Problem. We chase what walks in the door. Half our pursuit hours go to deals we were never going to win.
Solution.
  • Use data and history to find the signals that actually mean something.
  • Point growth leaders' limited time at the most qualified leads.
  • Concentrate effort where it moves the top line.
Outcome. The firm's most expensive hours land on the pursuits most likely to close, and win rates rise without burning out your leaders and teams.
Activate
Walk into the pitch already knowing the buyer
Problem. Every proposal team starts the research from zero, and we still reach orals knowing less than we should about the buyer's situation.
Solution.
  • Deliver a dossier for each live pursuit: the trigger, the buying committee, the context, and opening material.
  • The relationship-layer network map shows which growth leader has the warmest route in.
Outcome. Pursuit teams prep in minutes instead of days and arrive with a point of view, not a template.
Execution and expansion
Anchor and expand your existing accounts
Problem. Our best growth is inside accounts we already hold, and we leave it to chance. Cross-practice selling is clumsy, or we miss legitimate growth because no one was aware of the opening.
Solution.
  • Monitor the book for expansion and risk signals.
  • Surface cross-practice openings before they move past you.
  • Research what worked historically and the natural next services over an account's life.
  • Map the org and relationships to build presence across the client.
Outcome. Expansion becomes a deliberate motion instead of an accident, and at-risk accounts surface early enough to save.
The cycle is a loop, not a line. The bottom band feeds the top: execution and expansion done well compound into presence and reputation. The network graph built in the relationship layer is reused at Activate, so relationship capital is built once and used again.
